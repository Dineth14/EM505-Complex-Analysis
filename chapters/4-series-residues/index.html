Chapter 4

Series & Residues

Taylor and Laurent series reveal the local structure of analytic functions. The residue theorem transforms contour integrals into algebraic computations.

4.1 Taylor Series

(Taylor's Theorem) If $f$ is analytic in a disk $|z - z_0| < R$, then $$f(z) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{f^{(n)}(z_0)}{n!}(z - z_0)^n$$ The series converges absolutely and uniformly on compact subsets of the disk. The radius of convergence $R$ equals the distance from $z_0$ to the nearest singularity.

4.2 Laurent Series

(Laurent's Theorem) If $f$ is analytic in the annulus $r < |z - z_0| < R$, then $$f(z) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} a_n (z - z_0)^n$$ where $a_n = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma \frac{f(\zeta)}{(\zeta - z_0)^{n+1}}\,d\zeta$. The terms with $n < 0$ form the principal part.

4.3 Classification of Singularities

An isolated singularity $z_0$ of $f$ is:

  • Removable if the Laurent series has no principal part ($a_n = 0$ for $n < 0$)
  • A pole of order $m$ if $a_{-m} \neq 0$ and $a_n = 0$ for $n < -m$
  • An essential singularity if infinitely many $a_n \neq 0$ for $n < 0$

(Casorati–Weierstrass) Near an essential singularity, $f$ takes values arbitrarily close to any complex number. (Picard's theorem strengthens this: $f$ takes every value, with at most one exception, infinitely often.)

4.4 The Residue Theorem

The residue of $f$ at an isolated singularity $z_0$ is $$\text{Res}(f, z_0) = a_{-1} = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma f(z)\,dz$$ where $\gamma$ is a small circle around $z_0$.

(Residue Theorem) If $f$ is analytic inside and on a simple closed contour $\gamma$ except at isolated singularities $z_1, \ldots, z_k$ inside $\gamma$, then $$\oint_\gamma f(z)\,dz = 2\pi i \sum_{j=1}^{k} \text{Res}(f, z_j)$$

4.5 Computing Residues

Practical formulas for residues:

  • Simple pole: $\text{Res}(f, z_0) = \lim_{z \to z_0}(z - z_0)f(z)$
  • Pole of order $m$: $\text{Res}(f, z_0) = \frac{1}{(m-1)!}\lim_{z \to z_0}\frac{d^{m-1}}{dz^{m-1}}\left[(z - z_0)^m f(z)\right]$
  • $p/q$ rule: If $f = p/q$ with $q(z_0)=0$, $q'(z_0)\neq 0$, then $\text{Res}(f,z_0) = p(z_0)/q'(z_0)$

Key Takeaways

Taylor Series

Analytic functions equal their Taylor series in the largest disk free of singularities.

Laurent Series

Extends Taylor series to annular regions; the principal part reveals the singularity type.

Residue Theorem

$\oint f\,dz = 2\pi i \sum \text{Res}(f, z_k)$ — reduces integration to algebra.

Singularity Zoo

Removable (bounded) → pole (blows up) → essential (wild, Picard's theorem).
