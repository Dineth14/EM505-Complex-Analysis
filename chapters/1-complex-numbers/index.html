Chapter 1

Complex Numbers & the Complex Plane

We begin our journey by extending the real numbers to the complex plane. Here you will build geometric intuition for complex arithmetic, polar representations, and the elegant roots of unity.

1.1 The Complex Number System

A complex number is an expression of the form $z = x + iy$, where $x, y \in \mathbb{R}$ and $i^2 = -1$. We write $\text{Re}(z) = x$ and $\text{Im}(z) = y$. The set of all complex numbers is $\mathbb{C}$.

Complex numbers can be added and multiplied using the rules of algebra together with $i^2 = -1$:

$$(a + bi) + (c + di) = (a+c) + (b+d)i$$ $$(a + bi)(c + di) = (ac - bd) + (ad + bc)i$$

The modulus (absolute value) and argument (angle) of $z = x + iy$ are:

$$|z| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}, \qquad \arg(z) = \arctan\!\left(\frac{y}{x}\right)$$

(Euler's Formula) For any $\theta \in \mathbb{R}$, $$e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$$ Hence every complex number can be written in polar form: $z = r e^{i\theta}$ where $r = |z|$ and $\theta = \arg(z)$.

This connects the exponential function to trigonometry and reveals that multiplication by $e^{i\theta}$ is a rotation by angle $\theta$.

1.3 Complex Arithmetic — Geometric View

Addition of complex numbers follows the parallelogram rule: $z_1 + z_2$ is the diagonal of the parallelogram formed by the vectors to $z_1$ and $z_2$.

Multiplication is best understood in polar form. If $z_1 = r_1 e^{i\theta_1}$ and $z_2 = r_2 e^{i\theta_2}$, then:

$$z_1 z_2 = r_1 r_2 \, e^{i(\theta_1 + \theta_2)}$$

Multiplication multiplies moduli and adds arguments.

1.4 Roots of Unity

The $n$-th roots of unity are the $n$ solutions to $z^n = 1$. They are given by $$\omega_k = e^{2\pi i k / n}, \qquad k = 0, 1, \ldots, n-1$$ and form the vertices of a regular $n$-gon inscribed in the unit circle.

The roots of unity satisfy: (1) $\omega_k^n = 1$ for all $k$; (2) $\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \omega_k = 0$; and (3) $\prod_{k=0}^{n-1} \omega_k = (-1)^{n+1}$.

1.5 Topology of the Complex Plane

An open disk of radius $r$ centered at $z_0$ is $D(z_0, r) = \{z \in \mathbb{C} : |z - z_0| < r\}$. A set $U \subseteq \mathbb{C}$ is open if for every $z \in U$ there exists $r > 0$ such that $D(z, r) \subseteq U$.

A set is connected if any two points can be joined by a path lying entirely within the set. An open connected set is called a domain. Most of complex analysis takes place on domains.

(Triangle Inequality) For any $z_1, z_2 \in \mathbb{C}$, $$|z_1 + z_2| \le |z_1| + |z_2|$$ with equality iff $z_1$ and $z_2$ point in the same direction (i.e., $\arg z_1 = \arg z_2$).

Key Takeaways

Algebra ↔ Geometry

Addition is vector addition (parallelogram rule); multiplication is rotation + scaling.

Euler's Formula

$e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$ — the bridge between exponentials and trigonometry.

Roots of Unity

$z^n = 1$ has exactly $n$ solutions, equally spaced on the unit circle.

Domains

Complex analysis works on open connected sets — domains — in $\mathbb{C}$.
